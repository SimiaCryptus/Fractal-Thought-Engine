## ## The Static in the Kingdom of Woodchips

**Act 1, Scene 1**

**Setting:** The School Playground

**Characters:** Labubu, Milo

---

To Labubu, the playground was never merely a collection of bolted plastic and rusted chains. It was the Kingdom of Woodchips, a vast, aromatic empire where the cedar mulch held the scent of ancient libraries and the slide was the polished tongue of a sleeping silver giant.

Labubu crouched near the gnarled roots of the Great Oak, their long, serrated ears twitching at the secret frequencies of the wind. To the casual observer, Labubu was a jittery architecture of unsettling angles and far too many teeth—a blur of fur and chaotic mischief. But in their own mind, Labubu was a cartographer of the invisible. They were currently occupied with the sacred task of sorting "sun-stones"—pebbles that had drank enough light to feel pulse-warm against a palm—into a pattern that mirrored a constellation only Labubu could name.

The world was usually a vibrant symphony of primary colors and the rhythmic *thwack-thwack* of a tetherball. But then, the frequency shifted.

It began as a "hush."

In Labubu’s world, a hush wasn't just silence; it was a physical substance, a thick, gray static that drifted across the woodchips like a localized fog. It originated near the red climbing frame, where a cluster of children, led by Milo, had pulled themselves into a tight, vibrating knot.

Labubu’s ears swiveled. Usually, Milo was a source of "yellow" sounds—bright shouts, the clatter of a lunchbox, the chaotic music of a boy who ran faster than his laces allowed. But today, Milo was a muted indigo. He was hunched over, his hands moving in small, frantic gestures, his voice a sandpaper whisper that failed to carry its usual invitation.

Curiosity, a bright and itchy thing, tugged at Labubu’s chest. They abandoned the sun-stones and crept toward the climbing frame, their movements fluid and low to the ground, belly fur brushing the cedar.

"And we have to make sure the glitter doesn't leak," Milo was saying, his voice dropping an octave as Labubu’s shadow fell across the mulch. "If he sees the hats before Saturday, the whole thing is ruined."

Labubu stepped into the shade of the frame, eyes wide and hopeful. "Is it a secret about dragons?" Labubu asked, their voice a melodic rasp. "I have a map of the dragon tunnels under the sandbox. I can lend it to you, if the glitter needs a hiding place."

The effect was instantaneous. The "hush" solidified into a wall of ice.

The circle of children didn't just stop talking; they recoiled. It was a subtle, synchronized shift—a half-step back, a tightening of shoulders—but to Labubu, it felt like a sudden, violent drop in temperature. Milo, who usually laughed at Labubu’s stories of subterranean monsters, quickly shoved a crumpled piece of neon-pink paper into his pocket. His eyes, wide and darting, refused to lock onto Labubu’s.

"It’s nothing, Labubu," Milo said. His voice was thin, brittle as dry glass. "Just... school stuff. Boring stuff."

"Boring stuff doesn't usually require the alchemy of glitter," Labubu noted, their tail twitching with a rhythmic uncertainty. "Glitter is for celebrations. Or magic."

"It’s not for you," a girl named Sarah whispered. Milo nudged her sharply with his elbow, but the words had already landed, sharp and jagged.

Milo cleared his throat, staring at the ground where Labubu’s shadow stretched out, long and strange. "We’re just busy, okay? Go back to your rocks, Labubu. We’re doing something... private."

*Private.* The word tasted like copper on Labubu’s tongue.

Labubu stood frozen as the group began to drift away, their whispers starting up again like the buzzing of disturbed hornets. The static followed them, leaving Labubu standing alone in the center of the Kingdom. The sun-stones they had painstakingly collected now looked like ordinary pebbles—cold, gray, and profoundly silent.

Labubu reached out a clawed hand, as if to catch the tail end of the conversation, but there was nothing left but the smell of cedar and the stinging realization that the kingdom had grown very, very large, and they had grown very, very small.

The bell rang—a harsh, metallic scream that tore through the whimsical fog of the morning. As the other children raced toward the heavy school doors, Milo glanced back for a fleeting second. There was no wave, no secret signal. There was only a look of panicked guilt before he vanished into the building.

Labubu stayed behind for a moment, watching the neon-pink corner of the paper poke out of Milo’s pocket like a flicking tongue. The monster in the kingdom didn't feel like a king anymore. They felt like a ghost, haunting a place where the music had suddenly, inexplicably, stopped.

## ## The Rhythmic Heartbeat of Exclusion

**Act 1, Scene 2**

**Setting:** The Cafeteria

**Characters:** Labubu, Milo, Background Students

---

The cafeteria did not swallow Labubu; it inhaled her.

If the playground had been a kingdom of woodchips and whistling wind, the cafeteria was a cathedral of steam and staccato percussion. It was a cavern of yellow-tiled walls that sweated with the effort of containing three hundred screaming voices—a place where the air tasted of lukewarm gravy and the metallic tang of industrial floor wax. Usually, Labubu navigated this chaos like a small, furred creature in a thunderstorm, darting between the lightning strikes of dropped trays and the rhythmic thunder of sliding benches.

But today, the "hush" she had felt on the playground followed her through the double doors. It was a sticky, invisible thing, clinging to the hem of her sweater like a burr.

As she moved toward the lunch line, the roar of the room didn’t dim, but it *changed* shape. It curved around her, creating a pocket of dead air. When she passed the third-grade table, the laughter didn’t stop so much as it curdled, turning into a series of frantic, whispered coughs. 

Labubu clutched her plastic tray. It felt impossibly heavy, a rectangular shield she used to push through the thickening atmosphere. She looked for Milo.

He was at their usual table—the one near the tall windows where the dust motes danced in the light. But the light looked different today. It was no longer soft; it looked sharp, like shards of broken glass. Milo was surrounded by the others: Sarah, who smelled of strawberry lip balm and secrets; Toby, whose pockets were always bulging with forbidden trading cards; and the rest of the inner circle.

They were leaning in so closely their foreheads nearly touched, forming a human knot. In the center of that knot, Labubu saw a flash of that violent, neon pink.

She slowed her pace. Her heart, usually a frantic little bird, began to beat with a slow, rhythmic thud. *Thump. Thump. Thump.* It was the sound of a giant’s footsteps in a dark forest.

"…and my mom said we can have the glitter cannons in the backyard," Sarah’s voice drifted over the clatter of silverware, high and bright as a silver bell. "But she said we have to make sure the gate is locked so no one… you know… wanders in."

"Did everyone get the map?" Toby whispered, his voice thick with the thrill of a conspiracy. "The one with the X marking the 'No-Go Zone'?"

Milo didn’t speak. He was staring intensely at a puddle of chocolate milk on the table, tracing its jagged edge with a straw. He looked small. He looked like a boy who had swallowed a stone and was waiting for it to settle in his gut.

Labubu reached the edge of the table. The knot of children didn’t break; it tightened, a defensive formation of shoulders and elbows.

"Hi, Milo," Labubu said. Her voice felt strange in her throat—dry and ancient, like wind blowing through a hollow log.

The silence that followed was not a lack of sound. It was a physical weight. It pressed against Labubu’s chest, threatening to squeeze the air out of her lungs. Sarah looked up, her eyes wide and glassy, a fake smile stretching across her face like a rubber band pulled to the point of snapping.

"Oh! Hey, Labubu," Sarah said. Her hand moved with lightning speed, sliding a piece of neon-pink cardstock under her thigh. "We were just… talking about the math test. So boring, right?"

"The math test has glitter cannons?" Labubu asked. She didn't mean for it to sound like a challenge. She meant for it to be a bridge, a way back into the light.

Toby let out a sharp, nervous honk of a laugh. "It’s a… metaphor. For, uh, brain explosions. From the math."

Labubu looked at Milo. She waited for him to look up. She waited for him to say, *'Don't be silly, Sarah, tell her about the party.'* She waited for him to pull out a chair and tell her she was the guest of honor, that the "No-Go Zone" was actually a "Labubu-Only Throne."

Milo’s straw snapped. He looked up, but his eyes didn't meet hers. They skittered past her shoulder, focusing on a smudge of ketchup on the far wall.

"We’re kind of busy, Labubu," Milo muttered. The words were soft, but they hit her with the force of a physical blow. "It’s… it’s a private study group. For the… the boring stuff. You wouldn't like it."

"I like boring stuff," Labubu said, her voice trembling. "I like the way the dust grows on the bookshelves. I like the sound of the clock ticking when the teacher stops talking. I’m an expert at boring."

"Not this kind of boring," Sarah snapped, her patience finally fraying. "Just… go sit with the kindergartners or something, okay? We’re in the middle of something important."

The "hush" finally solidified. It became a wall of ice, clear and cold, separating Labubu from the table. She looked at the neon-pink paper peeking out from under Sarah’s leg. It wasn't just a party invitation. It was a map of a world where Labubu didn't exist.

She didn't cry. The tears felt too hot, too small for the coldness spreading through her limbs. Instead, she felt a strange, tectonic shift deep within her chest. The whimsical girl who saw dragons in the clouds and music in the rain began to recede, pulling back into the shadows of her own mind.

In her place, something else stirred.

It was the Monster. But it wasn't a monster with dripping fangs or glowing eyes. It was a monster made of sharp edges and quiet shadows. It was a monster that wore a party hat like a crown of thorns. If they wanted her to be the outsider, the one who "wandered in," then she would be exactly that. She would be the thing that haunted the edges of their bright, glittery world.

Labubu turned away. She didn't go to the kindergartners. She walked to the very back of the cafeteria, to a small, circular table that sat in the permanent shadow of the vending machines.

She sat down. She didn't open her milk. She didn't pick up her fork. She simply sat, her back straight, her eyes fixed on the knot of children across the room.

The cafeteria noise rushed back in, but Labubu was no longer a creature in the storm. She was the eye of it. She watched Milo laugh at something Toby said—a forced, jagged laugh—and she felt a flicker of something that wasn't sadness. It was clarity.

The Monster reached up and adjusted its invisible party hat. The celebration hadn't started yet, but Labubu knew one thing for certain: she was going to be the only one who knew how it ended.

## ## The Architect of Exile

**Act 2, Scene 1**

**Setting:** The School Playground

**Characters:** Labubu, Milo

---

The playground had soured. To the other children, it remained a familiar sprawl of sun-bleached plastic and splintered woodchips, but for Labubu, the geography had shifted into something jagged and hostile. The slide was no longer a dragon’s tongue; it was a border wall, slick and impassable. The swings were no longer pendulums of flight; they were heavy, rhythmic metronomes counting down the seconds of her erasure.

She stood by the chain-link fence, her fingers hooked into the cold, galvanized diamonds of the wire. She was a cartographer of her own exile, mapping the "No-Go Zones" that had sprouted like toxic fungi across the asphalt. 

Across the yard, near the rusted ribcage of the monkey bars, Milo was cornering Leo. Milo’s movements were twitchy, his shoulders hunched as if he were trying to retreat into his own ribcage. Labubu watched as he reached into his backpack and pulled out a sliver of neon-pink paper—the same electric hue that had scorched a hole in her heart earlier that morning. He handed it to Leo with a frantic, hushed urgency, a secret passed between conspirators. Leo’s face split into a wide, jagged expression of belonging as he tucked the invitation into his pocket. 

The "static" Labubu had felt earlier intensified. It wasn't just a sound anymore; it was a physical weight, a bruised twilight settling over her vision. She felt thin, like a pencil sketch someone had tried to rub out but hadn't quite finished. If she stayed "Labubu," she would eventually vanish entirely, worn away by the friction of being ignored.

*They don’t want the girl,* she thought, her gaze drifting toward the overflowing Lost and Found bin near the gym doors. *The girl is too much. The girl is "boring stuff." The girl is the wrong shape for the secret.*

She walked toward the bin, her movements stiff and deliberate. The air felt thicker here, smelling of damp wool, cedar, and the sour ghost of forgotten lunches. At the very top of the heap lay a trench coat—a massive, beige shroud that looked like it had belonged to a very tall, very lonely detective. Beside it sat a sturdy cardboard box, its flaps splayed open like the maw of a hungry cub.

Labubu reached out and touched the fabric of the coat. It was heavy, a skin of anonymity. 

She looked back at Milo. He was laughing now, but it was a brittle, glass-thin sound—the kind of laugh that shatters if you drop it. He looked happy, but he also looked small. He was safe inside the circle, but the circle was a cage.

A strange, cold heat began to radiate from Labubu’s chest. It was the "Monster" waking up—not a creature of nightmares that bit toes under the bed, but a Monster of Necessity. A creature that didn't need an invitation because it existed outside the laws of the Kingdom of Woodchips. 

If they wouldn't let her through the front door as a friend, she would arrive as an Event. 

She hauled the trench coat out of the bin. It felt like armor, weighted and wise. She picked up the box; it was the perfect size for a head—a square, unblinking head that she could paint with a single, cyclopean eye or a row of jagged, paper teeth. 

"You're making a mistake, Milo," she whispered to the wind, her voice no longer trembling. "You invited everyone but the most important guest."

She draped the coat over her arm. She would spend the afternoon in the garage, amidst the scent of sawdust and acrylic paint. She would build a new self, an architect of her own inclusion. She would be the shadow at the edge of the glitter cannons, the silence in the middle of the music.

Labubu wasn't a girl in a playground anymore. She was a blueprint. She was a storm in a party hat, and she was finally ready to celebrate.

## ## The Siege of the Citadel

**Act 2, Scene 2**

**Setting:** Milo’s House (The Fortress of Secrets)

**Characters:** Labubu

---

The garage was no longer a place for the family sedan or the skeletal remains of a lawnmower. To Labubu, it had become a cathedral of the obsolete, a laboratory for the unloved. It smelled of ancient sawdust, cold oil, and the sharp, metallic tang of a rusted bicycle chain. Beneath the rhythmic, dying hum of a flickering fluorescent bulb, the girl who had been erased from the guest list began her transmutation.

She draped the oversized trench coat across the workbench like a giant’s shed skin. It was a heavy, tan garment, thick with the scent of mothballs and the ghosts of rainy Tuesdays. Beside it sat the cardboard box, its flaps splayed open like the petals of a square, brown flower.

"A monster," Labubu whispered, her voice catching on the jagged edges of the silence. "A monster doesn't need an invitation. A monster simply *is*."

She worked with the surgical focus of a clockmaker. Using a thick black marker, she inked eyes onto the box—not round, friendly circles, but vast, swirling voids that looked as if they had swallowed the birth of stars. She carved jagged teeth from a discarded egg carton and glued them into a permanent, mocking grin. The adhesive was a sticky covenant, binding her rejection to the cardboard. 

Then came the crowning irony: the party hat. It was a relic from her own birthday two years ago, a cone of shimmering silver foil with a pom-pom that had long since lost its bounce. She perched it atop the box at a rakish, defiant angle. It was the mark of the beast, the signal that she was crashing the sun with a pocketful of shadows.

When she stepped into the costume, the world shifted. The trench coat dragged on the concrete, making her feel ten feet tall and infinitely heavy. The box settled over her head, plunging her into a world viewed through two narrow, rectangular slits. Inside, it smelled of recycled paper and her own hot, nervous breath. 

She was no longer Labubu, the girl with the quiet hands. She was the Architect of the Unseen.

The journey to Milo’s house was a pilgrimage through a neighborhood that had suddenly turned alien. The sidewalk was a narrow tightrope. Every rustle of a squirrel in the hedges felt like a witness to her transgression. She moved with a slow, rhythmic clanking—the sound of the coat’s heavy buttons striking the cardboard—feeling like a walking mountain of secrets. 

As she turned the corner onto Milo’s street, the "Citadel" came into view. 

Milo’s house was a bright, cheerful thing, painted the color of buttered toast. But today, it looked like a fortress. The windows were veiled with streamers that looked like festive entrails, and the front door was a mouth that swallowed children and spat out the muffled, distorted sounds of laughter. 

Labubu felt a sharp, cold pang in her chest. There was the driveway where they had once drawn chalk galaxies until their fingers were stained purple and blue. There was the porch where they had shared a thousand popsicles, racing to finish before the juice reached their elbows. Now, it was the No-Go Zone.

She didn't approach the door. Instead, she drifted toward the side of the yard, a shadow moving against the afternoon sun. She found her sanctuary behind the hydrangea bush—a massive, sprawling thing with blooms like clusters of blue brains. She sank into the dirt, the heavy fabric of the coat soaking up the dampness of the earth like a sponge. 

The leaves were cool against the cardboard of her head. Through her eye-slits, she watched the arrival of the "Chosen."

Toby arrived first, clutching a gift wrapped in paper so bright it hurt to look at. Then came Sarah, her hair tied in ribbons that fluttered like trapped butterflies. They didn't look like conspirators; they looked like children going to a party. That was the sting of it—the effortless, casual normalcy of their betrayal.

"Did you bring the glitter cannons?" she heard Toby shout as the door swung open.

Milo stood in the threshold. Even from her hiding place, Labubu could see the way he hovered, his shoulders hunched as if he were expecting a blow from the sky. He looked left, then right, his eyes scanning the street with a frantic, guilty energy. He was looking for her, she realized with a jolt of electricity. He was looking for the girl he had left behind, never imagining she was already there, watching from the heart of the blue brains.

## ## The Siege of the Citadel

The hydrangeas were no longer a sanctuary; they had become a cage of damp leaves and judgmental shadows. Inside her cardboard skull, Labubu’s breath was a rhythmic, humid ghost, fogging the narrow eye-slits she had jaggedly carved with safety scissors. For an hour, she had watched the Citadel—Milo’s house—glow like a fallen star dropped into the middle of the suburbs.

The house hummed. It was a low-frequency vibration of bass, high-pitched shrieks of laughter, and the rhythmic *thwack* of a screen door that seemed to be breathing guests in and out. To Labubu, it wasn't just a party; it was a machine designed to manufacture her absence.

"Now," she whispered, the word rattling against the interior of the box. "The Monster arrives."

She stood, and the transition was agonizingly slow. The oversized trench coat, heavy with the scent of mothballs and forgotten winters, dragged against the mulch like a tired tide. She adjusted the silver party hat—her jagged, metallic horn—which sat precariously atop the box. She felt immense. She felt ridiculous. She felt like a tectonic plate shifting under the weight of a secret.

She began her pilgrimage across the lawn. Each step was a deliberate act of war against the manicured grass. The Monster did not walk; it lumbered. The coat flapped around her ankles, and the cardboard head wobbled, giving her the appearance of a top-heavy specter. Through the slits, the world was a fragmented mosaic of golden window light and obsidian dark.

As she reached the porch, the music grew louder—a frantic, sugary pop song that felt like a slap to the face. She could smell it now: the buttery scent of popcorn, the chemical tang of fruit punch, and the unmistakable aroma of belonging.

Labubu reached for the brass handle. Her hand, swallowed by the cavernous sleeve of the coat, fumbled for a moment. Her heart was a bird trapped in a chimney, soot-stained and frantic. *They will scream,* she thought with a dark, shivering glee. *They will see the thing they tried to bury in the silence.*

She didn't knock. The Monster had no use for etiquette.

Labubu threw her weight against the door. It swung open with a violent, cinematic groan, hitting the interior wall with a crack that cut through the music like a guillotine.

The transformation of the room was instantaneous.

Labubu stood in the doorway, a towering, box-headed anomaly draped in a shroud of thrift-store wool. The silver party hat glinted under the chandelier, casting jagged reflections across the ceiling. Silence poured into the room, heavy and suffocating.

Milo was standing near the snack table, a plastic cup frozen halfway to his lips. His eyes, wide and reflecting the silver of her horn, weren't filled with the terror Labubu had rehearsed. Instead, they were filled with a terrible, shimmering recognition.

Toby and Sarah, the lieutenants of the in-crowd, stood paralyzed near the sofa. A handful of popcorn fell from Toby’s hand, clicking softly against the hardwood floor like tiny, edible hailstones.

Labubu’s breath hitched. The Monster was supposed to roar. It was supposed to demand an explanation for the exile. But standing there, under the judgmental glow of the domestic sun, the cardboard felt thin. The trench coat felt like a shroud rather than armor.

She looked at the room—the streamers, the cake, the warmth—and realized she was a smudge of charcoal on a white silk dress. She was the glitch in the perfect program.

"I'm here," she wanted to say, but the words were trapped behind the cardboard.

Milo took a single, tentative step forward. His face was a mask of tense confusion, his brow furrowed as if he were trying to solve a puzzle that had suddenly come to life and walked into his living room.

"Labubu?" he whispered, his voice barely audible over the ringing in her ears.

The Monster trembled. The siege had been successful; the Citadel was breached. But as Labubu looked at the sea of bewildered faces, she realized she had no idea what to do with the ruins.

## ## The Birthday of the Beast

**Act 3, Scene 2**

**Setting:** Milo’s House (The Fortress of Secrets)

**Characters:** Labubu, Milo, The "In-Crowd" (Classmates)

---

The silence in the living room was a pressurized chamber, a thick curtain that muffled the world outside. Labubu stood rooted to the center of the rug, a cardboard golem draped in a trench coat that smelled of mothballs and ancient, trapped rain. Inside the box, her breath was a rhythmic, humid rasp against the walls. She waited for the laughter. She waited for the "In-Crowd" to point their fingers at the intruder, the freak, the girl who had mistaken a secret for a snub.

Milo took a step forward, his sneakers chirping against the hardwood. He didn’t look like a king in his citadel; he looked like a boy who had tried to swallow a secret too large for his throat.

"Labubu?" he whispered. "You’re... you’re early."

The word *early* hit her with the force of a physical blow. It didn't fit the script of her martyrdom. She had prepared for *late*. She had prepared for *never*.

"I saw the lights," Labubu’s voice came out muffled, vibrating within the cardboard. "I saw the invitations. I saw the way you all whispered in the hallways like I was a ghost you were trying to exorcise."

Milo’s eyes widened. He shot a panicked look back at Toby and Sarah, who were frozen near the sofa, clutching a crumpled roll of butcher paper like a weapon.

"We weren't trying to keep you out," Milo said, his voice hitting a frantic, high-pitched edge. "We were trying to keep you *away*. Just for an hour! Toby, the tape! Sarah, hit the lights!"

Before Labubu could retreat—before the Monster could flee back into the safety of the night—the room plunged into darkness. For a heartbeat, she was back in the void, the familiar isolation wrapping around her. Then, a chorus of voices, uncoordinated and joyous, erupted.

"SURPRISE!"

The lights flickered back on, blindingly bright. Toby and Sarah lunged upward, unfurling the butcher paper. It wasn't a map of her exclusion; it was a riot of glitter, neon markers, and crooked, loving lettering.

*HAPPY BIRTHDAY TO THE MONSTER QUEEN,* it read.

Labubu froze. The "In-Crowd"—the golden children she had spent weeks envying and fearing—weren't wearing masks of mockery. They were wearing cheap, conical party hats, the elastic bands pinching their chins. Sarah held a plate of cupcakes decorated with frosting that looked suspiciously like purple fur. Toby held a bag of confetti, his face flushed a deep, embarrassed red.

"We’ve been planning this for a month," Milo said, rubbing the back of his neck. "That’s why we were whispering. You’re too observant, Labubu. You’re like a detective. We had to act like we were ignoring you just to keep the secret. I thought I’d ruined everything when you saw me at the store yesterday."

The "Fortress of Secrets" wasn't a prison built to keep her out. It was a temple built in her honor.

The weight of the trench coat suddenly felt unbearable—not because of its heaviness, but because of its absurdity. The "Monster" she had created was a suit of armor for a war that wasn't happening.

Slowly, Labubu reached up. Her fingers, trembling slightly, gripped the edges of the cardboard box. With a soft, suctioning sound, she lifted it off.

The cool air of the living room hit her sweaty forehead like a benediction. Her hair was a static-charged mess, sticking to her cheeks, and her eyes were watery from the sudden light. She looked at Milo, then at the banner, then at the sea of faces that were no longer a "crowd," but a group of children waiting for her to join them.

"I thought..." she started, her voice cracking. "I thought I wasn't invited."

"Not invited?" Sarah laughed, stepping forward to offer a cupcake. "Labubu, the party *is* you. We even made the snacks weird on purpose. Look, these pretzels are 'Bones of the Fallen.'"

Milo walked over, his expression softening into a shy, genuine smile. He reached into his pocket and pulled out a party hat—not a standard one, but one he had clearly spent hours on, covered in faux fur and two small, felt horns.

"You’re the only one who gets the crown," he said, handing it to her.

Labubu looked down at the hat, then back at the box head lying discarded on the rug like a shed skin. The "Monster" was gone, but the "Beast" remained—the whimsical, strange, and deeply loved version of herself that her friends had seen all along.

She took the hat, perched it between her messy pigtails, and felt the last of the cold triumph melt into a warm, radiating glow.

"Okay," Labubu said, a small, mischievous smile tugging at her lips. "But I’m keeping the trench coat. It has excellent pockets for 'Bones of the Fallen.'"

The room erupted into laughter, and for the first time, Labubu didn't feel like she was watching the world through a window. She was the one holding the glass.
