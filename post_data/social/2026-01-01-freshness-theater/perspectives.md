The text correctly identifies 190°F (87°C) as "culinary malpractice" for *consumption*, but it is important to distinguish between **brewing temperature** and **holding/serving temperature**.
*   **Brewing:** The Specialty Coffee Association (SCA) mandates a brewing temperature of 195°F–205°F (90°C–96°C). This is necessary to dissolve the desired solids (lipids, acids, sugars).
*   **Serving:** The optimal temperature for the human palate to perceive the complex flavor profile of coffee is between **120°F and 140°F (49°C–60°C)**. 
*   **The Technical Failure:** When coffee is held at 190°F, it undergoes "thermal degradation." The chlorogenic acids break down into quinic and caffeic acids, which are intensely bitter and astringent. Volatile aromatic compounds—the "nose" of the coffee—evaporate rapidly at these temperatures, leaving behind a flat, "burnt" liquid.

#### B. Sensory Masking and the TRPM5 Channel
There is a physiological reason why "Idiot Culture" prefers extreme heat: **Thermal Masking**. The TRPM5 taste-sensing protein channel in human taste buds is highly temperature-dependent. 
*   At 190°F, the heat triggers pain receptors (nociceptors) that effectively drown out the taste buds. 
*   This is a technical "hack" for low-quality beans. If a chain uses over-roasted, low-grade Robusta beans, serving the coffee at a "habitable" 140°F would reveal the defects (moldy, rubbery, or overly bitter notes). By serving it at 190°F, the institution ensures the customer cannot actually *taste* the product until it has cooled, by which time the palate has been desensitized.

#### C. The Thermodynamics of the "Drive-Thru Buffer"
From an industrial engineering perspective, the 190°F service temperature is a "buffer" against the **Rate of Cooling**. 
*   In a drive-thru model, the system assumes a 15–20 minute "commute latency." 
*   Standard paper cups have poor R-values (insulation). A cup served at 190°F will drop to a drinkable 150°F in approximately 10–15 minutes depending on ambient temperature. 
*   The "Theater" is therefore a technical solution to a logistics problem: ensuring the coffee is still "hot" when the customer reaches their office, even at the cost of the beverage's chemical integrity during the first ten minutes.
