## ## The Grey Pasture

**Act 1, Scene 1**

**Setting:** The Meadowbrook Hills (The Decaying Pastures)

**Characters:** Bo Peep, Corrupted Sheep

---

Meadowbrook Hills had once been a tapestry of rolling emerald, a place where the wind carried the honeyed scent of clover and the rhythmic, peaceful chime of copper bells. Now, the hills were a corpse, unburied and festering.

Bo Peep stepped through the knee-high grass, though it no longer felt like vegetation. It was brittle, calcified, and coated in a fine, grey silt that puffed up like funeral ash with every footfall. Above, the sun was a pale, cataract-filmed eye struggling to pierce a shroud of violet mist. The air tasted of wet iron and old copper—the suffocating, metallic tang of a storm that refused to break.

She gripped her grandmother’s crook, the polished rowan wood smooth against her calloused palms. It was a relic of a gentler age, a tool designed for guidance and the tender rescue of the lost, not for the grim geography of this new world. Her heart hammered a frantic, uneven rhythm against her ribs, a living pulse in the suffocating silence of the valley.

"Daisy?" she whispered. Her voice felt thin and fragile, swallowed instantly by the hungry fog.

She remembered Daisy as a lamb—a frolicking puff of white wool with a stubborn penchant for clover blossoms. Bo followed the trail of trampled, blackened silt toward the skeletal remains of a weeping willow near the creek. The water didn't babble anymore; it churned sluggishly, thick and dark as bile, dragging against the banks like liquid lead.

A shape huddled beneath the willow’s drooping, leafless branches. 

"Daisy, girl, it’s me," Bo said, her voice trembling with a hope she knew was a lie. She reached out a hand, her fingers twitching. "I’ve come to bring you home. The fold is waiting. The lamps are lit."

The shape stirred. It didn't rise with the clumsy, heavy grace of a ewe. It uncoiled. 

As the creature turned, Bo’s breath hitched, dying in her throat. The wool was no longer white; it was a matted, oily grey, fused with shards of obsidian-like bone that protruded from the spine like jagged teeth. But it was the eyes that broke Bo’s heart. They weren't the gentle, horizontal slits of a prey animal. They were weeping orbs of necrotic violet, glowing with a predatory, senseless hunger that transcended mere starvation.

The creature opened its mouth. Instead of a bleat, a harrowing, metallic screech tore through the mist—the sound of rusted shears grinding against a whetstone.

"Oh, Nana," Bo whispered, her knuckles whitening on the rowan wood. "What has the world done to you?"

The corrupted ewe lunged. 

It moved with a terrifying, jerky speed, its joints popping like dry kindling. Bo reacted on instinct, swinging the crook to ward the beast off. She had spent years using this staff to hook stray lambs, to pull them from the briars. But as the wood connected with the creature’s shoulder, there was no soft give of wool and muscle. It was like striking a statue made of iron.

The wood groaned. With a sickening, final *crack*, the ancient rowan snapped in two. 

The impact sent Bo sprawling into the grey silt, the breath driven from her lungs. The creature was on her in an instant, its breath smelling of rot and fermented bile. Its teeth, now jagged and elongated into serrated needles, snapped inches from her throat. 

Panic flared, hot and sharp, but beneath it, a cold, ancestral steel took hold. It was the marrow-deep resolve of a woman who had spent her life protecting the helpless from the wolves. She reached for the small of her back, her fingers closing around the hilt of her skinning knife. It was a tool of the trade—meant for fleece and butchery, for the maintenance of life and the necessity of the harvest.

As the monster bared its throat for a final, crushing strike, Bo drove the blade upward with every ounce of her desperation. 

The knife sank into the creature’s neck with a wet, heavy thud. Instead of bright red blood, a viscous, black ichor sprayed across Bo’s face, stinging her eyes like acid. The creature let out one last, distorted wail—a sound that was almost a sob—before collapsing on top of her, its weight unnaturally heavy, like a sack of wet stones.

Bo pushed the carcass off, gasping for air that tasted of soot. She wiped the black sludge from her eyes and looked down at the thing that had been Daisy. The corruption hadn't just changed the sheep; it had hollowed it out, replacing its spirit with something hateful and hollow.

She looked at the broken halves of her grandmother’s crook lying in the ash. The symbol of the shepherd was gone, splintered and useless in the dirt. 

A low hum began to vibrate through the ground, a sub-bass thrum that rattled her teeth. Bo stood up, her legs shaking, and looked out into the violet mist. The fog shifted, swirling in the wake of a sudden, cold draft that smelled of the deep earth. 

Dozens of violet lights flickered to life in the gloom. Pairs of glowing, necrotic eyes stared back at her from the ridges, from the creek bed, and from the shadows of the dead trees. The metallic screeching began to rise in a discordant, echoing chorus, a wall of sound that promised no mercy.

Bo Peep didn't reach for the broken wood. She gripped the blood-slicked handle of her knife and reached for the whetstone at her belt. The sound of steel on stone rang out, sharp and defiant against the encroaching dark.

This wasn't a plague to be cured. This wasn't a flock to be gathered. 

It was a harvest. And she was the only one left to reap it.

## ## The Lavender and the Iron

**Act 1, Scene 2**

**Setting:** The Ancestral Fold

**Characters:** Bo Peep

---

The heavy oak door of the Ancestral Fold slammed shut, the iron bolt sliding home with a finality that vibrated through the cottage’s stone bones. Outside, the discordant screeching of the flock rose in a cacophonous tide—a sound of wet slate dragged over teeth, stripped of anything resembling a bleat. Inside, the air was a stagnant vault of preserved memory: dried lavender, lanolin, and the ghostly, biting sting of her grandmother’s pipe tobacco.

Bo Peep leaned her weight against the timber, her breath coming in ragged, shallow hitches that burned her throat. The black ichor of the thing that had been Daisy was already hardening on her skin, tightening like a necrotic second hide. She looked down at her hands. They were shaking—not with the tremors of a girl in terror, but with the violent, rhythmic vibration of a soul being hammered into a harder, colder shape.

The cottage was a museum of a life that had ended ten minutes ago. There were the spinning wheels, silent and skeletal in the shadows; the jars of preserved rowan berries on the mantle, glowing like dull embers; the hand-woven tapestries depicting the rolling green hills of a Lowland currently being swallowed by a tide of ink. It was a sanctuary of peace, but as Bo looked at the splintered rowan crook she had leaned against the wall, she knew peace was a luxury the dead could afford. The living had to pay in iron.

"I'm sorry, Nana," she whispered into the dim light. Her voice sounded thin, a stranger’s plea. "The crook wasn't enough."

She moved to the hearth. The fire had long since collapsed into grey ash and cold cinders. Bo knelt, the hem of her blue pastoral dress dragging through the soot, staining the lace. She didn't feel the chill. She reached for the heavy iron poker and wedged it into the seam of the largest hearthstone—the one etched with a weathered, ancient carving of a lamb curled within a lion’s mane.

With a grunt of exertion that strained the cords of her neck, she pried the stone upward. The rock groaned, resisting the intrusion, before flipping back to reveal a shallow, silk-lined cavity. 

Inside lay a bundle wrapped in oil-blackened leather.

Bo reached in and pulled the weight of it onto her lap. As she unwrapped the hide, the temperature in the room seemed to plummet. The scent of lavender vanished, smothered by the sharp, ozone tang of ancient magic and the cold, predatory bite of well-oiled steel.

*The Shepherd’s Wrath.*

It was not a sword for a knight, nor a tool for a soldier. It was a heavy, single-edged blade, curved with the cruel elegance of a reaper’s scythe but balanced for the brutal efficiency of the thrust. Runes were etched deep into the fuller, glowing with a faint, rhythmic amber light that pulsed like a dying heartbeat. Her grandmother had told her stories of this steel—how it had been forged not to tend the sheep, but to defend the fold when the wolves were no longer made of flesh and bone.

Bo ran a finger along the flat of the blade. The metal was cold enough to sear. 

*“A shepherd’s job is to keep the flock,”* Nana’s voice rasped in the hollows of her memory, grave and unyielding. *“But a shepherd’s duty is to know when the flock is lost. When the rot takes the wool, you do not pray, Little Bo. You reap.”*

Bo stood, the blade feeling unnaturally right in her hand, as if it had been waiting for her grip to mature. She moved to the washbasin, splashing freezing water over her face and arms, scrubbing at the black blood of the ewe. She watched the water turn murky and dark, swirling down the drain like a fading nightmare she refused to wake from. 

She stripped. The blue dress, the white apron, the silk ribbons that had bound her golden hair—they fell to the floor in a heap of useless finery, an effigy of the girl she used to be. She stepped out of the persona of the shepherdess who waited for the sun to rise. 

From a cedar chest at the foot of her bed, she pulled out her true inheritance. These were not the clothes of a girl who sang to the hills. There was a gambeson of thick, boiled leather, reinforced with iron studs at the shoulders and chest. There were bracers etched with the same protective runes as the blade, and heavy, mud-caked boots built for the jagged teeth of the high mountain passes. 

She dressed methodically, her movements precise and devoid of wasted energy. Each buckle she cinched, each strap she tightened, felt like a seal being placed upon her old life. She bound her hair back with a strip of raw leather, pulling it tight until the skin at her temples pulled, anchoring her focus. 

Finally, she sat on the edge of the hearth and picked up the whetstone. 

The screeching outside had shifted. It was no longer a chaotic chorus; it had become a rhythmic, heavy thumping against the stone walls. They were testing the perimeter, tasting the mortar, smelling the blood on the wind.

*Scree. Scree. Scree.* 

The sound of the stone against the edge was a counterpoint to the scratching at the door. Bo wasn't thinking about the sheep anymore. She wasn't thinking about the soft warmth of their wool or the way they used to nuzzle her palms for salt. She thought of them as meat. She thought of them as a harvest of corruption. She thought of Bellwether, the great alpha ram, and the way his eyes had dissolved into pits of burning sulfur.

The grief that had threatened to drown her in the pasture had calcified. It was a hard, cold stone in her gut now—a weight she could carry, a weapon she could use. 

She stood up, *The Shepherd’s Wrath* humming in her grip. The amber light of the runes cast long, dancing shadows against the cottage walls, making the spinning wheels look like instruments of torture. She looked at the door. The heavy oak was beginning to splinter, the wood groaning under the force of a rhythmic, monstrous ramming.

Bo Peep didn't flinch. She adjusted her grip on the hilt, feeling the leather wrap bite into her palm, welcoming the pain. She wasn't a girl looking for lost sheep. 

She was the butcher. And the flock was overdue for the blade.

She walked toward the door, her boots heavy and certain on the floorboards, and reached for the bolt.

## ## The First Harvest

**Act 1, Scene 3**

**Setting:** The Ancestral Fold (Exterior/Threshold)

**Characters:** Bo Peep, The Corrupted Flock

---

The sun was a guttering candle, spilling bruised purples and sickly golds across the lowland horizon. Inside the cottage, the air had curdled. It tasted of ancient dust and the sharp, electric tang of whetted steel. Bo Peep stood before the heavy oak door, her knuckles white where they gripped the hilt of *The Shepherd’s Wrath*. The wood groaned, the iron hinges shrieking as something massive and mindless hammered against the exterior.

This was no longer the sanctuary of her childhood. It was a cage, and the things baying outside were no longer her charges.

A splintering crack detonated through the room. A massive, ossified horn—yellowed and ridged like a mountain crag—punched through the center of the door. It was followed by the sickening squelch of necrotic flesh and a spray of black, oily bile. The bleating that followed was a discordant harmony of a thousand wet, rattling throats—a sound that didn't just grate on the ears; it stripped the spirit bare.

Bo Peep didn't retreat. She felt the cold, heavy legacy of her grandmother vibrating in her palm. The sword was too heavy for a girl of her stature, yet it balanced perfectly, as if seeking the center of her gravity. She leaned in, her lips brushing the freezing steel of the crossguard, and whispered the word her grandmother had once hummed as a lullaby—a word not of peace, but of pruning.

*"Sunder."*

The runes etched into the blade didn't merely glow; they ignited. A pale, predatory violet light bled from the metal, casting long, dancing shadows that seemed to claw at the walls. The sword hummed, a low-frequency thrum that resonated in Bo’s marrow. It wasn't just a weapon; it was a hungry thing, a tool forged for a specific, terrible harvest.

The door exploded inward in a spray of splinters.

Three rams tumbled into the room, their bodies swollen with unnatural, corded muscle. They were nightmares of geometry—extra limbs sprouted from their flanks like jagged, leafless branches, and their wool was matted into a foul armor of filth and dried blood. Their eyes were nothing more than pits of swirling, milky cataracts. The lead ram, a beast that might have once been a prize-winner named Jasper, lunged with a speed that defied its bulk.

Bo moved.

She didn't move like a girl; she moved like a pendulum, precise and inevitable. She stepped inside the ram’s guard, the violet light of the blade carving a crescent moon through the dim air. The steel met the creature’s neck, and there was no resistance. The blade didn't just cut; it unmade. Where the edge passed, the corruption evaporated into a foul-smelling mist, leaving behind only the dry, brittle remains of what the rot had consumed.

The ram collapsed, its head hitting the floorboards with the hollow, pathetic thud of a dried gourd.

The other two were on her instantly. One caught her shoulder, its jagged, multi-rowed teeth tearing through the leather of her tunic and drawing a hot line of fire across her skin. Bo grunted, the pain grounding her, turning her fear into a cold, sharp edge. She spun, using the momentum to drive the heavy pommel of the sword into the second ram’s snout, shattering bone into wet grit. As it reeled, she brought the blade down in a vertical arc, splitting the third beast from crown to brisket.

The room fell into a momentary, ringing silence, save for the predatory sizzle of the blade as it finished "digesting" the filth on its edge.

Bo stood amidst the remains, her breathing shallow and rhythmic. She looked at her hands. They weren't shaking. The terror that had paralyzed her in the pasture with Daisy had been replaced by a crystalline focus. She felt the blade’s hunger through the hilt—a demand for more. The corruption wasn't just a plague; it was a crop that had grown too tall, and she was the only one left with the scythe.

She stepped over the threshold, treading upon the shattered remains of her front door.

The valley below was a sea of shifting shadows. Thousands of glowing, cataract-white eyes stared back at her from the darkness of the fold. The sunset had vanished, leaving the world in a shroud of grey moonlight. The wind carried the scent of the entire flock—thousands of them, all turned, all waiting for the command of the alpha, Bellwether.

Bo Peep looked out over the land her family had tended for generations. The stone walls she had helped her father repair, the streams where she had washed the wool—it was all a graveyard now. She felt a pang of grief, sharp and sudden, but she pushed it down into the cold hollow where her fear used to live.

She raised *The Shepherd’s Wrath*, the violet runes casting a ghostly halo around her silhouette. She was no longer the girl who had lost her sheep. She was the one who had found them, and she knew exactly what they were worth.

"The harvest has begun," she whispered to the wind.

She stepped off the porch and into the tall grass, no longer a shepherdess, but the Shepherd of the End.

## ## The Last Shepherd

**Setting:** The Weeping Crag / The Meadowbrook Hills (View from above)

**Characters:** Bo Peep

The silence was not an absence of sound; it was a physical weight. It settled over the summit of the Weeping Crag like a burial shroud, thick and suffocating, muffling the world below. 

Bo Peep lay on her back, her breath hitching in a chest that felt crushed by iron bands. Each inhalation was a jagged blade of frost carving its way down her throat. Above, the sky was beginning to bleed. The deep, bruised purple of the mountain night was surrendering to a sickly, translucent grey—the false dawn of the lowlands, cold and unforgiving.

Beside her, the thing that had been Bellwether was finally still.

The corruption did not vanish in a cinematic flash of light. It died the way a fire dies when the fuel is spent—slowly, with a lingering, foul-smelling smoke. The oily, black veins that had pulsed through the ram’s flesh lost their luster, turning from a rhythmic, obsidian glow to the dull, matte grey of cooling slag. The Great Horns, once jagged spires of nightmare that seemed to pierce the very fabric of reality, began to calcify. They became brittle, porous, and pathetic, shrinking back into the skull of a common beast.

Bo watched through stinging eyes as the Rot receded from the earth itself. The weeping moss of the Crag, which had bled a dark, viscous ichor during the struggle, began to dry and flake away into ash. The unnatural, feverish heat that had radiated from the hive-mind dissipated, replaced by the biting, honest chill of the high-altitude air.

She forced herself to sit up. Her left shoulder screamed in protest, a hot, throbbing reminder of the ram’s final, desperate charge. Her fingers, slick with a mixture of her own blood and the black discharge of the corruption, gripped the shaft of her silver-tipped crook. It was bent now, the elegant curve of the head notched and scarred. It was no longer a tool for guiding the lost; it had become a weapon for silencing the damned.

"It’s over," she whispered. Her voice sounded small, a fragile, tinny thing easily swallowed by the vastness of the peaks.

She looked down at Bellwether. In death, the monstrous proportions melted away, leaving behind the skeletal remains of a creature that had once been her favorite. She remembered him as a lamb, wobbling on spindly, uncertain legs, his wool as white as a summer cloud. She had fed him from a bottle when his mother had rejected him, feeling the frantic, hopeful beat of his heart against her palm. She had named him for the bell he wore—a bright, rhythmic sound that used to signal safety, the coming of dusk, and the warmth of the fold.

Now, there was no bell. There was only the wind whistling through the hollows of his ribs.

Bo stood, her legs trembling with a fatigue that went deeper than muscle. She limped to the edge of the Crag, her heavy, mud-caked boots crunching on stone that felt like bone. From this height, the Meadowbrook Hills stretched out like a rumpled green quilt, slowly being revealed by the creeping, grey light of morning.

The corruption was fading there, too. She could see the dark stains on the distant pastures turning to grey ash, carried away by the morning breeze. The forests, which had been choked with the black, strangling vines of the Rot, seemed to exhale, their silhouettes softening against the horizon. The world was safe. The lowlands were preserved.

But the pastures were empty.

There were no white dots moving across the emerald hills. No rhythmic, distant bleating echoed up from the valleys. The silence she had fought so hard to achieve was absolute. She had saved the land, but she had been forced to hollow it out to do so. 

She thought of her grandmother’s stories—of the Shepherdess as a figure of mercy, a light in the dark, a gatherer of the strayed. Bo looked at her hands. They were the hands of a butcher. She had not gathered her flock; she had harvested them. She had looked into the milky, corrupted eyes of Daisy, Bramble, and Pip, and she had ended them. She had been the last thing they saw, and she had offered them no grace but the cold edge of a blade.

A solitary tear tracked through the grime on her cheek, carving a pale path through the soot and dried blood.

"I am the last," she said, the realization settling into her bones like the mountain’s frost.

She was nineteen years old, and she was the last shepherd of the Meadowbrook. But a shepherd without a flock was something else entirely. She was a sentry. A ghost. A wolf who wore the skin of a girl to keep the true monsters at bay. 

She turned away from the view and looked back at the summit. She would not leave the bodies to rot. Even now, with the corruption gone, they deserved the dignity of the earth. It would take her all day, perhaps longer, to bury them in the thin, rocky soil of the Crag. It would be her final act as their mistress—to tuck them into the ground where the Rot could never find them again.

Bo Peep picked up her broken crook. She didn't lean on it for support; she held it like a scepter. The lethal calm that had carried her through the slaughter had not left her. It had simply solidified, turning into a permanent architecture of her soul. The girl who had lost her sheep was gone; the woman who had killed them remained.

She would go back down, eventually. She would walk through the empty village, past the silent pens and the hollow houses. She would tell the people of the lowlands that the nightmare was over, that they could sleep without fear. But she knew she would never truly belong among them again. You could not kill everything you loved and then sit by a hearth as if the smoke didn't smell like burning wool and ancient sin.

The sun finally broke over the horizon, a sliver of cold gold that hit the silver tip of her crook, making it flash like a dying star. 

Bo Peep stood tall against the wind, a solitary silhouette against the dawning world. The flock was gone, but the Shepherd remained. And as long as she breathed, the shadows would think twice before creeping back into the light.

She knelt by Bellwether’s head, ignored the agony in her shoulder, and began to dig.
